5 Essential Warm-Up Exercises for Dancers

TIPS November 5, 2025 3 min read

Every dancer knows that a proper warm-up is the difference between a great practice and an injury. At Gobind Dance Academy, we start every class with a sequence designed to prepare the body for the specific demands of Nepali and Bollywood dance movements.

These five exercises form the foundation of our warm-up routine. They target the areas most used in dance — the hips, spine, ankles, and shoulders — while also building the mind-body connection that is essential for expressive performance.

1. Neck and Shoulder Rolls

Nepali and Bollywood dance place heavy demands on the neck and shoulders. From the distinctive neck movements of Kathak-inspired choreography to the sharp shoulder accents in folk dances, this area needs careful preparation.

Start by standing with feet hip-width apart and arms relaxed. Slowly roll your neck in a full circle, five times clockwise and five times counterclockwise. Follow with shoulder rolls — lift both shoulders toward your ears, roll them back, and release. Repeat ten times in each direction. This increases blood flow to the upper body and releases tension held in the trapezius muscles.

2. Hip Circles and Figure Eights

Hip mobility is essential for almost every dance form GDA teaches. From the grounded movements of Nepali folk to the energetic isolations of Bollywood, the hips are the engine of the dance.

Place your hands on your hips and trace a wide circle with your pelvis — ten times clockwise, ten times counterclockwise. Then transition into figure eights, moving the hips in a smooth infinity pattern. This mobilizes the hip joint and activates the core stabilizers that protect your lower back during intense choreography.

A cold muscle is a vulnerable muscle. Our warm-up is designed not just to raise the heart rate, but to wake up every joint and muscle group we will use in class. There are no shortcuts.

3. Ankle Mobilization

Folk dances from Nepal involve intricate footwork — from the heel-toe patterns of the Dhime to the rapid weight shifts of the Maruni. Ankle injuries are one of the most common issues among dancers, and prevention starts with proper mobilization.

Lift one foot off the ground and trace the alphabet in the air with your toes. Switch feet and repeat. Then, standing on both feet, slowly rise onto your toes and lower back down, twenty times. This strengthens the stabilizer muscles around the ankle and improves balance.

4. Spinal Rolls and Cat-Cow

A supple spine is the hallmark of a well-trained dancer. Many Nepali and Bollywood movements require articulation of the spine, from deep backbends in expressive passages to the flat-back positions in folk formations.

On all fours, alternate between arching your back (cow pose) and rounding it (cat pose), moving slowly with your breath. Follow this with standing forward rolls — start standing, tuck your chin, and roll down one vertebra at a time, then roll back up. Repeat eight times.

5. Dynamic Leg Swings

High kicks, deep lunges, and rapid directional changes are common in both Nepali folk and Bollywood choreography. Preparing the hamstrings, hip flexors, and glutes is critical.

Hold onto a wall or barre for support. Swing one leg forward and back ten times, then side to side ten times. Keep the standing leg slightly bent and maintain an upright torso. Repeat on the other leg. This dynamic stretch prepares the legs for explosive movement while maintaining the stability required for controlled choreography.

Practice this warm-up before every class or rehearsal, and your body will thank you. At GDA, we believe that discipline in preparation creates freedom in performance. The stage is where you shine, but the warm-up is where you build the foundation.
