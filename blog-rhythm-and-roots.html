Rhythm & Roots: The Music Behind the Movement

MUSIC October 12, 2025 5 min read

Before the body can move, there must be rhythm. In Nepali dance, the music is not merely an accompaniment — it is the source from which the movement is born. Every step, every gesture, every expression is a response to a complex tapestry of percussion, melody, and vocal tradition.

At Gobind Dance Academy, we believe that a great dancer must also be a great listener. Understanding the music is not optional — it is foundational. This article explores the traditional instruments and rhythmic structures that form the backbone of Nepali dance.

The Madal: Heartbeat of Nepal

The madal is arguably the most important instrument in Nepali folk music. This double-headed hand drum, slung across the player's shoulder, produces a warm, resonant tone that perfectly complements the human voice and the dancer's footwork. Its two heads — one tuned higher, one lower — create a call-and-response quality that drives much of Nepal's traditional dance music.

In GDA classes, students learn to recognize the different madal rhythms associated with specific dances. The steady dhakka beat of the Jhyaure folk rhythm, the rapid chayra pattern of celebration dances, and the solemn bhajan tempo of devotional pieces all require different physical responses from the dancer.

The madal is not just an instrument — it is the heartbeat of our culture. When a dancer truly hears the madal, their feet move in conversation with it, not in competition.

The Sarangi: Voice of Sorrow and Joy

The sarangi is a bowed string instrument that produces a haunting, vocal-like sound. Played with a horsehair bow, its four main strings and dozens of sympathetic strings create a rich, resonant texture. In Nepali dance, the sarangi often carries the melody during slower, more expressive sections — the storytelling passages where emotion takes precedence over footwork.

Dancers trained at GDA learn to listen for the sarangi's cues. When the sarangi takes the lead, the dance shifts from percussive footwork to flowing, narrative gesture — the hands telling stories while the feet remain grounded.

Other Essential Instruments

  • Dholak — A larger double-headed drum, common in Bollywood and North Indian folk. Its deeper, more resonant bass gives dance music its driving energy.
  • Bansuri — The bamboo flute, whose soaring melodies evoke the mountains and hills of Nepal. Used in both folk and semi-classical dance compositions.
  • Khartal — Wooden clappers used in devotional music. Their crisp, percussive sound marks rhythmic accents in traditional dance.
  • Jhyali — Bronze cymbals that provide the bright, shimmering texture in Newari ceremonial music.
  • Damphu — A frame drum unique to the Tamang community, played during the Tamang Selo dance — a fast, energetic form popular in Nepali festivals.

Rhythm Cycles in Nepali Dance

Nepali music, like other South Asian traditions, is built on rhythmic cycles called taal . The most common cycle in Nepali folk dance is the Dadra taal, a six-beat cycle that naturally suits the walking and stepping patterns of traditional dance. Other cycles include the 16-beat Tintal (common in Kathak-influenced choreography) and the 8-beat Keherwa (widely used in Bollywood dance numbers).

At GDA, our students practice identifying these cycles by ear from the first class. We believe that rhythmic literacy is as important as movement literacy. A dancer who can feel the sam (the first beat of a cycle) without counting is a dancer who has truly internalized the music.

The relationship between music and dance in Nepali culture is one of mutual respect. Neither leads — they walk together, each responding to the other. At GDA, we teach our students to hear that conversation and to add their voice to it through movement.
